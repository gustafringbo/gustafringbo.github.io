Dokk1 micro-events

Dokk1 is a library and an open cultural institution that encourages community, learning, and play for all ages. Visitors can have a difficult time, however, arranging micro-events. Micro-events can be described as spontaneously arranged events, or events that require low amounts of organization. This was the case presented to us digital design students by the cultural manager of Dokk1, with a five day project deadline to present a prototype and the design-process.

Micro-event board

After five intense days we presented our physical lo-fi prototype, which can best be described as a physical magnetic micro-event schedule. The prototype has a grid layout with time-slots, and magnetic event cards, some with prewritten events to inspire guests, and some blank that can be filled out with whiteboard markers. When a card is placed on a time-slot, a small location tracker is released from it. The location of the tracker is shown on a map above the grid schedule, so arrangers can find a suitable area after adding their micro-event to the board.

ideation at DOKK1

My role in the project

In this project, I worked in a group of four, along with three other digital design students. Due to time pressure we had to work fast, and as a result I held a multitude of changing roles, though mostly worked as UX designer on the project. I worked with research methods, such as desktop research, semi-structured interviews, and evaluation. I also facilitated an ideation workshop, evaluation, and planned the before- and now-scenario. Later in the process, I worked on the development of both prototype iterations, and the design of the interface. In the end we presented our solution to highly satisfied professors and case-presenters, who saw great potential in the idea.

The process

The process was highly cooperative, and the entire group therefore took part in all stages of it. Through the entire project I took on the role of the facilitator of the group, as all other members had a more careful approach. I did my best to foster open communication and high cohesiveness, and making sure we didn't get stuck on an issue or detail. In the end, the design was a solution that was easy to use for all ages, inspired visitors to arrange events, and was easy to notice.

Region Midtjylland & inclusion

Different health workers from Region Midtjylland presented a design case consisting of the issues of inclusion in the health sector. One of these issues lies in the handling of homeless people by hospitals especially, where their systems are not built to handle patients with no address. Thus, homeless patients are not able to be treated with proper dignity and professionalism, and are not able to be monitored or contacted after being discharged.

Discharge report re-design

My team's solution to the problem, was to focus on the issues experienced by nurses in the current discharge system, and design an alternative. As the problem was complex and sensitive, a larger coordinated solution would be required, where institutions could cooperate with groups like the existing "street team", to monitor people where they are. to design an intuitive and user friendly interface, that could be integrated, we decided to begin a complete re-design of the current discharge report, MedCom. Our design offered fewer interactions, much clearer visual hierarchy and communication, and widgets to make interaction easier, compared to the old system.

My role in the project

During the project, I was in had the responsibility for managing the project, planning our work, and keeping focus on the task, while also doing UX studies and interface design. I participated in an important ethnographic field study, where a previously homeless person offered a guided tour of the streets of Aarhus, while telling us about life as a homeless person in the city. I further facilitated an divergent ideation workshop, worked on our interactive prototype and facilitated an interview with the head nurse of Randers Hospital.

The process

In the early stages of our process, we were unsure of how a digital solution would be able to make a difference. In many areas, the issue we were dealing with, would be better solved with improvements to social or political areas. After much research, we decided our expertise was best suited to improve the current out-dated discharge system used by nurses. The entire medical communications system was complex and sub-optimal according to nurses and our own observations. We therefore began a complete re-design of patient discharge, that assumed cooporation possibilities with existing medical organisations that specialize in dealing with homeless people. Though implementation would require a re-design of MedCom entirely and structural expansions of the communication network, it would provide improved UX for nurses and possibilities for better monitoring and more dignified treatment of homeless people.

Employees from Region Midtjylland presented a wide array of issues concerning inclusivity in the health sector. We chose to focus on the sensitive issue of homeless people, particularly when discharging them, as current systems are not built to handle this. Our solution was a rework of the current discharge report interface, with an alternative path for patients with no address. I was highly involved with qualitative etnographic studies, and managing the agile project phases and team efforts. Large areas of the issue were, however, discovered to not be requiring non-technological solutions.
